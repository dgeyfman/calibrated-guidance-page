Calibrated Test-Time Guidance for Bayesian Inference

Daniel Geyfman*, Felix Draxler*, Jan Groeneveld*, Hyunsoo Lee, Theofanis Karaletsos, Stephan Mandt

ICML 2026

Existing test-time guidance methods for diffusion models don't recover the correct posterior distribution. We propose estimators that enable calibrated sampling.

How it Works

Calibrated Bayesian Guidance (CBG) produces a direction to take each diffusion step that is consistent with the true Bayesian posterior. We propose a gradient-free estimator that works by drawing samples from the true diffusion posterior and evaluating their likelihoods. Then, we compute a likelihood-weighted average of the samples to give a direction for the diffusion step while avoiding gradients.

Why standard guidance can fail

To sample from the Bayesian posterior, guidance should use the diffused likelihood: an average of the likelihood over samples from the true diffusion posterior p(x | xt). Existing methods use approximations to avoid this integral, such as Tweedie's mean estimate or a Gaussian approximation. These approximations are biased, so adding more compute makes them converge more confidently to the wrong distribution rather than to the true Bayesian posterior.

Fast sampling

CBG relies on drawing candidate samples from the diffusion posterior p(x | xt) at each denoising step, but doing this with a full inner diffusion sampler can be extremely expensive. In the case where we are working with closed-form likelihoods and distributions, we can build an analytic sampler directly, but when scaling up to images, this is not possible. We propose an alternative solution that uses one-step or few-step models to approximate these posterior samples. We use deterministic one-step models x=ft(xt) , and sample from the stochastic posterior using renoising.

Benchmarking CBG

Across several diverse benchmarks, CBG gives better performance than other test-time guidance methods, and improves performance as the compute budget increases.

Bayesian Inference

On five Bayesian inverse-problem benchmarks with reference samples from the true posterior, CBG improves distributional accuracy as the likelihood-evaluation budget increases. Both gradient-free and gradient-based CBG move toward the optimal C2ST value, while other test-time guidance methods generally plateau because their diffused-likelihood approximations remain biased.

Black-hole Imaging

We apply CBG to a scientific inverse problem, reconstructing black-hole images from radio-telescope measurements. Using the gradient-free estimator, CBG produces reconstructions that remain visually consistent with the ground truth and reaches stronger PSNR as the number of per-step posterior samples increases.

Image Inverse Problems

We also apply CBG to two image tasks: 4x super-resolution and prompt alignment. In super-resolution, it reconstructs 256 × 256 images from 64 × 64 inputs by using the low-resolution observation as a likelihood. In prompt alignment, it uses a vision-language reward model to guide text-to-image generation toward exact constraints in the prompt.
